Save Your Tooth, Relieve Your Pain

Few dental procedures have been as unfairly misrepresented as root canal treatment. For decades, the phrase "root canal" has conjured images of an agonizing ordeal — but the reality in modern dentistry is almost the opposite. At Long Lake Family Dentistry, root canal therapy is performed with excellent local anaesthesia and a gentle, precise technique that most patients find far more comfortable than they expected. In fact, the discomfort you feel before a root canal — from the infected or inflamed tooth — is almost always far worse than the procedure itself.

💡 The Truth About Root Canals

Root canals don't cause pain — they relieve it. The procedure removes the infected or inflamed nerve tissue that is the source of your pain, leaving you comfortable and your natural tooth intact. Studies consistently show that patients who have had root canal treatment rate it as no more uncomfortable than having a routine filling placed.

Modern root canal therapy is precise, gentle, and highly effective at saving teeth and eliminating pain.

A root canal becomes necessary when the soft tissue inside the root of a tooth — called the pulp — becomes infected or severely inflamed, typically due to deep decay, a crack in the tooth, or trauma. Left untreated, the infection spreads, destroying the surrounding bone and potentially leading to a painful abscess and the eventual loss of the tooth. Root canal therapy removes the infected pulp, cleans and seals the root canals, and allows you to keep your natural tooth — which is always preferable to extraction from a health and functional standpoint.

Signs You May Need a Root Canal

🔥 Severe, persistent toothache — especially with pressure or heat
🌡️ Prolonged sensitivity to hot or cold that lingers after the source is removed
🔴 Swelling or tenderness in the gum near the tooth
💛 Darkening or discolouration of the tooth
🫧 A recurring pimple or bump on the gum (dental abscess)
😶 A tooth that was once painful but has gone completely numb

What to Expect During Treatment

1

Diagnosis & X-Rays

We examine the tooth and take digital X-rays to assess the extent of infection and the anatomy of the root canals. This guides our treatment plan and ensures precision throughout the procedure.

2

Local Anaesthesia

The area is thoroughly numbed with local anaesthetic. We take our time to ensure you are completely comfortable before proceeding — you should feel pressure but no pain during the treatment.

3

Pulp Removal & Canal Cleaning

A small opening is made in the crown of the tooth. Using specialized instruments, we carefully remove the infected pulp tissue, then clean, shape, and disinfect each root canal to eliminate all bacteria.

4

Sealing the Canals

The cleaned canals are filled and sealed with a biocompatible material called gutta-percha, which prevents future bacterial contamination. A temporary or permanent filling is then placed.

5

Crown Placement

Most teeth that have had root canal treatment benefit from a dental crown to protect and strengthen the tooth long-term. We will discuss crown options and schedule this as a follow-up appointment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a root canal hurt? +
With modern anaesthesia and technique, root canal treatment is typically no more uncomfortable than having a filling. You may feel pressure or vibration but should not feel pain. After the procedure, some mild soreness is normal for a few days and is easily managed with over-the-counter pain relief.
Why not just extract the tooth instead? +
Keeping your natural tooth is almost always the better long-term choice. Extractions lead to bone loss at the site, neighbouring teeth shifting over time, and the need for a replacement (implant or bridge) that carries its own cost and complexity. A successfully treated root canal tooth can last a lifetime.
How many appointments does it take? +
Most root canals are completed in one or two appointments. Simple cases are often done in a single visit. More complex cases — particularly molars with multiple canals or severe infections — may require two appointments. A crown placement is then scheduled as a separate visit.
Is root canal treatment covered by dental insurance? +
Root canal treatment is a restorative procedure and is typically covered by dental insurance, though the percentage varies by plan. Most plans cover 50–80% of the cost after your deductible. We recommend confirming your coverage before your appointment and our team can assist with that process.
What happens if I don't get the root canal? +
A tooth with infected pulp will not heal on its own. The infection will spread to surrounding bone and tissue, potentially forming a painful abscess. Eventually the tooth will need to be extracted. Addressing the problem promptly with root canal therapy is almost always the safest, most cost-effective, and least disruptive option.
